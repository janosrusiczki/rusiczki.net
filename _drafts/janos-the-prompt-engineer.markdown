Is this what it means to be a developer in 2026? I'm an AI coding agent herder telling them what to do and checking their output for hallucinations? Maybe. And I'm not even complaining!

But first a little bit of history about Janos' interactions with AI, or to be more precise LLMs and coding agents.

I'm not sure exactly when I signed up for the Github Copilot trial but I got an email on the 17th of August 2022 that my trial is about to end. The 5th of September was a $0 free plan setup and the first real payment came on the 8th of September. I remember I haven't used it during the trial and I got charged for it which gave me the motivation to start testing it out and while doing that I thought I'd give the competition a chance. So on the 18th of September 2022 I signed up for the Tabnine trial and I somehow forgot to cancel that too. After the 14-day trial ended, on the 2nd of October 2022, I was charged for a $144 yearly subscription. I contacted them that same day and got refunded the next day. Tabnine felt inferior to Copilot anyway, so I stayed with the latter since then.

And then came the end of 2022 and the beginning of 2023 when ChatGPT took the world by storm. I remember writing prompts and marveling at the output and soon enough noticing Stack Overflow having a banner which instructed their users that they shouldn't post ChatGPT generated answers. That was the beginning of the end for Stack Overflow, which is a bit unfair since I'm pretty sure ALL of the coding helpers were trained on their content. On the other hand, I remember my frustration not being able to use SO to ask something like: "I have X and Y and want to accomplish Z. What is the best approach? What are the best libraries?" The question would be locked in a short time for being "subjective". So I was very happy as soon as I could converse about my questions with LLMs that would actually give me answers.

A bit later in 2023 I got into the GitHub Copilot Chat private beta on the 7th of July. It seemed out of this world: I wasn't merely getting code completions, I could ask natural language questions and the LLM could respond having the context of my code. I didn't even need to copy relevant stuff over into ChatGPT anymore. Wowzers!

Then at the beginning of 2024 Google Gemini (finally) launched and in May 2024 I even got myself Gemini Advanced for a short while, which I used with more or less success to edit a Romanian article about [a surprise find in my yard](https://www.rusiczki.net/2024/09/25/narnia-din-curtea-mea/) while doing some DIY. It was funny that I only asked it to proof read that article and edit it to avoid repetitions and other writing blunders I'm guilty of but it somehow went off the rails and rewrote half of my article in Spanish. I was amused. The rest of the articles around here are 100% certified AI free though. Pinky promise!

**Update while editing:** What I said was true when I started writing this article. Since then I published four rapid fire articles (the latest four before this article) augmented with Claude. I think I'll write a separate article about how my writing process for this blog changed, until then, you can rest assured that what you're reading was only augmented with AI - I still edit the shit out of the LLMs output manually.

Around the 13th of December 2024 I added [CodeRabbit](https://www.coderabbit.ai/) to my Photonia project. It's an AI code reviewer that leaves comments on your pull requests, it was free for open source, and it got there well before GitHub Copilot did. This was before agentic development was a thing for me, so I applied its suggestions by hand. It occasionally stalled me on a feature entirely: procrastination (too lazy to apply the fixes) versus OCD (couldn't ignore them). Funny thing is, at some point CodeRabbit added agent-ready prompts for each suggestion. But I didn't fully get those back then, however...

A few months later, on May 26, 2025, the AI coding agents era started for myself also — [Emil](https://www.emilburzo.com) recommended [OpenRouter](https://openrouter.ai/) with [aider](https://aider.chat/) and I was off. I signed up with OpenRouter, topped up $10 and successfully added some features to my [open source project](https://github.com/photonia-io/photonia). I was quite amazed. Then I checked OpenRouter stats and I saw that [Kilo Code](https://kilo.ai/) appeared a lot. So, obviously, I installed Kilo Code and tried it out. I liked that it was integrated into VSCode (which is my editor of choice), that I could resume earlier sessions, that it had multiple modes for different tasks (Architect for planning, Coder for coding, Debugger for debugging), that I could write my own rules, and that it showed me the cost of my session on the fly.

Then I switched jobs in September 2025 and the new client encouraged the use of AI coding assistants. We had pretty frequent training sessions and I learned quite a few things which would have passed me by. One of the first trainings was about how to write better prompts, and soon after a page appeared on the work Confluence listing recommended MCP servers. This was also the moment I started working with Claude, as the client was pushing for [Claude Code](https://claude.com/product/claude-code).

Later in the autumn, Kilo Code became the first tool where I started experimenting with MCP servers on my own projects (at work I was already using them through Claude Code, as mentioned above). MCP servers are basically small add-ons that let an AI agent reach external tools, APIs, and data beyond the code in your editor. From this point on the ball really started rolling, moments of amazement ("OMG, it can do that?") were coming very frequently.

I topped up my OpenRouter account again with $20 on October 21st and another $20 on November 29th. As I'm writing this I still have $3.27 left.

Got a recommendation for [z.ai](https://z.ai) from [Andrei](https://www.linkedin.com/in/andrei-verdes/) but I pretty much ignored it for a while. On the 6th of November 2025, the same Emil said that I should get myself a Claude Pro account. I did but it quickly turned out that it wasn't the wisest choice. 5 hour limit, weekly limit, limits upon limits. Luckily I got a beta test for web-based Claude Code, which gave me $250 to have fun with until November 18 so I tried doing as much as I could. Around those same days, I also rediscovered how good GitHub Copilot had become. It could route huge features through Claude models for just one premium credit per task, which was a lifesaver when I was hitting Claude Pro's limits. Bonus: it picked up the same rules I'd defined for Kilo Code, so no extra setup.

On the 7th of November, literally the day after, I finally took Andrei up on his recommendation and signed up for z.ai's GLM Coding Lite plan at $9 (first-month promo). Three weeks later, on the 28th of November, I upgraded to GLM Coding Pro for $29.15 (prorated, via a $30-for-3-months promo), not because of quota but because Pro's responses were noticeably faster.

Also on the 6th of November 2025, I got a hint from, you guessed it, Emil for [Perplexity](https://perplexity.ai), that I should associate it with PayPal and I got Perplexity Pro, which is a sort of Google on steroids. I use Perplexity for researching any type of tech acquisition I make. So for example, I researched a projector, graphics cards. I like that it searches the web for current information and actually crafts a response from the findings. I also use it to write copy for websites I have by researching and synthesizing information from different websites.

On the 7th of December I signed up for a Kilo Code account and topped up $20, and I immediately received a $20 bonus credit. The advantage over OpenRouter was that Kilo Code doesn't add tax on top, and they also offer free models. That's what kept me going with them for a few months.

For a long time, AI was just a helper for me. I used it for things that would take me ages on my own, or that aren't my strong points, like UI work. Copilot autocomplete and chat helped a lot, but I was still writing each and every change by hand. All of that was happening on a single personal project, Photonia. Then came the job switch, and the workflow slowly shifted. I kind of embraced the idea that I'd do everything through AI agents. The amount of code I write by hand these days is basically zero. I spend most of my dev time conversing with agents, and I first see the code when I'm reviewing a pull request.

That same switch also got me to fan out: I started a handful of new projects that had been sitting on my "would love to build this someday" list for years. Some were know-how gaps. Others were just time.

The first was a Jekyll media manager, scratching an itch from when I [switched this blog from Wordpress to Jekyll in 2018](https://www.rusiczki.net/2018/01/08/a-new-blogging-engine/). I literally wrote back then that I was "badly missing" Wordpress' media manager. This one was never a know-how problem. I could have built it whenever. It was a time problem. AI helped by being fast: it drafted the app and we iterated until I had something very close to an MVP. I still haven't released it, but I really should finish it up and start using it on the blog.

When Google [Antigravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google_Antigravity) launched on the 18th of November 2025, I got inspired and sketched out an isometric city/colony management browser game I'd been mulling over for ages. ADD confirmed. :D I only spent a few hours on it over a couple of days, and what I have is really just a sketch that doesn't even have a repo, but I was amazed I could sketch it at all. I was missing two things here: how a game should actually be put together (state machines and friends), and how to program an isometric one specifically.

Around the end of November I started on a news clusterer. Another idea I'd been sitting on for years because I had no idea how to cluster text. Then Kilo Code shipped a new feature that indexed your codebase into a [qdrant](https://qdrant.tech/) database. I looked up what qdrant was, saw in the first paragraph that it was great for clustering text, and it clicked. Most of the work happened between Christmas and New Year's Eve, while I was off work and Claude was running [a holiday promotion](https://support.claude.com/en/articles/13163666-holiday-2025-usage-promotion) that doubled the usage limits on my Pro account. The normal Pro limits felt very constricting, but 2x was enough to get a lot of news clusterer work done that week. I even built an Android TV app for it, because why not. It's live at [stiri.link7.ro](https://stiri.link7.ro), still chugging along, gathering local Maramureș news and clustering it into storylines.

Finally, I decided to revive [Hai Afară!](https://haiafara.ro) ("Come Outside!" in Romanian), an app to upload, edit, and showcase hiking and biking trails. I'd first worked on it back in 2018 and then abandoned it for the same reason: I didn't have the know-how to finish it. For this one I upgraded to Claude Max 10x on the 2nd of February 2026, and I've been on it ever since. That's also the real reason my Kilo Code subscription didn't end up being my daily driver despite all the good things I just said about it.

Once I had Claude Max I didn't really need z.ai anymore (except for the occasional agentic code review), so I handed the keys to my wife. She had a blast with it and finally tackled some QA automation tasks she'd been putting off. She even reimbursed me for the z.ai renewal on the 28th of February ($90). :)

Within that agent workflow, three kinds of work stand out. The biggest is stuff I could never finish in a reasonable amount of time on my own because it needs know-how I don't have and don't want to pick up just for one feature. [Related Tags](https://github.com/photonia-io/photonia/pull/1008) in Photonia is my go-to example. It's a feature that suggests tags which commonly appear together on public photos, using co-occurrence metrics (support, confidence, lift, Jaccard) precomputed nightly via Sidekiq and exposed through GraphQL. That's straight data mining, and there is zero value in me learning association rule learning from scratch just to ship one feature. I planned the whole thing with Claude Opus through OpenRouter's API, then implemented it with z.ai the day after I signed up for their plan. Plan with one tool, build with another. The other two things are more mundane: boilerplate repetitive stuff I was procrastinating on (ten minutes of actual work, nine months of avoidance), and writing and adjusting unit tests, which is the exact thing I know I should always do and almost never want to.

It's not just coding, either. I've used Claude Code to reorganize my homelab (configuring and debugging Docker containers), to set up and secure my VPS, and lately for blogging help, including the article you're reading right now. But I never got around to using autonomous agent wrappers like [OpenClaw](https://openclaw.ai/), which can run Claude Code sessions on their own, open PRs, and resolve errors while you sleep. I don't trust AI blindly enough for that.

A few things I've picked up along the way:

- Garbage in, garbage out, in a very specific sense: vague prompts produce vague code. Writing better prompts is half the job now, maybe more.
- You really do have to stay in the loop. Agents aren't trustworthy enough yet to leave alone. Someone has to pump the brakes when they start going in circles or cheerfully reinforcing a bad pattern, and that someone is me. See also: why I haven't gone near OpenClaw.
- Back when I was on API-based billing through OpenRouter and Kilo Code, there was a weird incentive I kept running into: the providers reward fast follow-up responses because of prompt caching. Respond within the 5-minute cache window and you pay a fraction of the token price. I am not naturally fast at that, so I was probably leaving money on the table. Less of a concern now on Claude Max 10x, which is flat-rate. Though I've hit the Max limit twice in about two and a half months, both times on huge refactors, so "unlimited" is a relative term.
- For my ADD brain, all of this has been a welcome boost. So many new toys. Every week there's a new agent, a new model, a new workflow. As you can see, this was a pretty short period since I discovered agentic AI, but it was packed with changes. I'm not sure how sustainable it is for my attention span, but while it lasts I'm having fun.

I'm pretty sure the *financial* AI bubble will burst, I just hope this doesn't mean that they will take away my toys.
